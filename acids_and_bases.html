Introduction to Acids and Bases

This chapter expands on your foundational knowledge of acids, bases, and salts from Forms One and Two. It delves deeper into the chemical principles governing their behavior and properties.

Learning Objectives

  • Provide modern definitions for acids, bases, and salts.
  • Differentiate between strong and weak acids and bases based on their degree of ionization.
  • Explain why the choice of solvent is critical for a substance to exhibit acidic or basic properties.
  • Quantitatively compare the strength of acids and bases using pH and electrical conductivity measurements.
  • Confidently write and interpret formula and ionic equations for neutralization and precipitation reactions.
  • Define solubility and apply solubility curves to practical scenarios like salt extraction.
  • Describe the chemical causes of temporary and permanent water hardness and the methods to remove them.

Strength of Acids and Bases

The strength of an acid or a base is determined by the degree to which it ionizes or dissociates in water. We can compare their strengths by measuring their pH values and electrical conductivity.

Acids

Strong acids ionize completely in water, releasing a high concentration of hydrogen ions (\(H^+\)). Weak acids only ionize partially, establishing an equilibrium.

Strong Acid Example: \(HCl_{(aq)} \rightarrow H^+_{(aq)} + Cl^-_{(aq)}\)

Weak Acid Example: \(CH_3COOH_{(aq)} \rightleftharpoons H^+_{(aq)} + CH_3COO^-_{(aq)}\)

Worked Example: Comparing Acid Strengths

In an experiment, 2M hydrochloric acid (\(HCl\)) and 2M ethanoic acid (\(CH_3COOH\)) are compared. Which acid is stronger and why?

Answer: Hydrochloric acid is the stronger acid.

Reasoning:

  • pH Value: Hydrochloric acid has a lower pH value (e.g., pH 1) compared to ethanoic acid (e.g., pH 3-4). A lower pH indicates a higher concentration of \(H^+\) ions.
  • Electrical Conductivity: The \(HCl\) solution has a higher electrical conductivity because it contains a greater concentration of mobile ions (\(H^+\) and \(Cl^-\)) to carry the electric current. This is a direct result of its complete ionization.

Bases and Alkalis

An alkali is a base that is soluble in water. Strong alkalis dissociate completely in water to produce a high concentration of hydroxide ions (\(OH^-\)).

Strong Alkali Example: \(NaOH_{(aq)} \rightarrow Na^+_{(aq)} + OH^-_{(aq)}\)

Weak Alkali Example: \(NH_{3(g)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons NH_{4(aq)}^+ + OH^-_{(aq)}\)

Worked Example: Comparing Alkali Strengths

Compare the strength of 2M sodium hydroxide (\(NaOH\)) and 2M ammonia solution (\(NH_3\)).

Answer: Sodium hydroxide is the stronger alkali.

Reasoning:

  • pH Value: Sodium hydroxide solution has a higher pH value (e.g., pH 14) than ammonia solution (e.g., pH 10-11), indicating a higher concentration of \(OH^-\) ions.
  • Electrical Conductivity: \(NaOH\) solution has higher conductivity due to the high concentration of \(Na^+\) and \(OH^-\) ions from complete dissociation.

Role of Solvents

For a substance to show acidic or basic properties, it must be able to form ions. The solvent plays a critical role in facilitating this ionization.

  • In Polar Solvents (like Water): Dry hydrogen chloride (\(HCl\)) is a molecular compound. When dissolved in water, the polar water molecules attract the hydrogen and chlorine atoms, breaking the covalent bond and allowing ionization: \(HCl_{(g)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_3O^+_{(aq)} + Cl^-_{(aq)}\). The presence of hydroxonium ions (\(H_3O^+\)) gives the solution its acidic properties.
  • In Non-Polar Solvents (like Methylbenzene): When \(HCl\) gas is dissolved in a non-polar solvent, it remains as simple molecules. No ionization occurs, so there are no free hydrogen ions to give the solution acidic properties. It will not react with metals or turn litmus paper red.

Safety Note

Methylbenzene (toluene) is volatile and poisonous. All experiments involving it must be conducted in a fume hood.

Amphoteric Substances

Amphoteric oxides and hydroxides are versatile substances that react with both acids and strong alkalis.

Common examples are oxides/hydroxides of Zinc (\(Zn\)), Aluminium (\(Al\)), and Lead (\(Pb\)).

Reaction with Acid (acting as a base):

\(ZnO_{(s)} + 2H^+_{(aq)} \rightarrow Zn^{2+}_{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)}\)

Reaction with a Strong Alkali (acting as an acid):

\(ZnO_{(s)} + 2OH^-_{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow [Zn(OH)_4]^{2-}_{(aq)}\) (Tetrahydroxozincate(II) ion)

Salts and Solubility

Determining Solubility

Solubility is the maximum mass of a solute that dissolves in 100 g of a solvent at a specific temperature to form a saturated solution.

Worked Example: Calculating Solubility

A saturated solution of a salt weighs 80g. After evaporating the water, the mass of the dry salt is 30g. Calculate the solubility of the salt.

Answer:

1. Find the mass of the solvent (water):
Mass of solvent = 80g - 30g = 50g.

2. Calculate solubility per 100g of water:
Solubility = \( \left( \frac{30 \text{ g}}{50 \text{ g}} \right) \times 100 = 60 \text{ g/100 g of water}\)

Solubility Curves and Fractional Crystallisation

A solubility curve graphically represents how solubility changes with temperature. This is applied in fractional crystallisation to separate a mixture of salts. When a hot, concentrated solution of the mixture cools, the salt that is less soluble at lower temperatures will crystallise out first.

Water Hardness

Hardness in water is caused by dissolved Calcium (\(Ca^{2+}\)) and Magnesium (\(Mg^{2+}\)) ions, which react with soap to form scum.

\(2C_{17}H_{35}COONa_{(aq)} + Ca^{2+}_{(aq)} \rightarrow (C_{17}H_{35}COO)_2Ca_{(s)} + 2Na^+_{(aq)}\)

Types and Removal of Hardness

1. Temporary Hardness

Cause: Calcium hydrogencarbonate (\(Ca(HCO_3)_2\)).

Removal by Boiling: \(Ca(HCO_3)_{2(aq)} \rightarrow CaCO_{3(s)} \downarrow + H_2O_{(l)} + CO_{2(g)}\)

2. Permanent Hardness

Cause: Calcium sulphate (\(CaSO_4\)) etc.

Removal by Na₂CO₃: \(CaSO_{4(aq)} + Na_2CO_{3(aq)} \rightarrow CaCO_{3(s)} \downarrow + Na_2SO_{4(aq)}\)

Key Experiments

Experiment 1.2: Comparing pH and Electrical Conductivity

Objective: To compare the strength of different acids.

Procedure: A universal indicator is added to solutions of 2M HCl and 2M CH₃COOH to measure pH. The electrical conductivity of each solution is measured using an ammeter.

Observations: HCl solution has a lower pH and causes a larger deflection on the ammeter than the CH₃COOH solution.

Conclusion: HCl is a stronger acid because it ionizes more completely, producing a higher concentration of H⁺ ions.

Experiment 1.4: Hydrogen Chloride in Different Solvents

Objective: To investigate the role of the solvent in acidic character.

Procedure: HCl gas is dissolved separately in water (a polar solvent) and methylbenzene (a non-polar solvent). Both solutions are tested with litmus paper and by adding a piece of magnesium ribbon.

Observations: The aqueous solution turns blue litmus red and reacts with magnesium to produce effervescence. The methylbenzene solution shows no reaction.

Conclusion: Water is required for HCl to ionize and exhibit acidic properties.

Experiment 1.7: Determining Solubility of a Salt

Objective: To measure the solubility of a salt (e.g., KClO₃) at a specific temperature.

Procedure: A saturated solution is prepared. A known mass of the saturated solution is weighed, then evaporated to dryness. The mass of the remaining dry salt is measured.

Calculation: Mass of water = (Mass of solution) - (Mass of dry salt). Solubility (g/100g water) = (Mass of dry salt / Mass of water) x 100.

Experiment 1.12: Investigating Water Hardness

Objective: To compare the effect of different dissolved salts on the lathering of soap.

Procedure: A standard volume of soap solution is added drop by drop to different water samples (distilled, tap, and solutions of various salts like MgCl₂ and Ca(HCO₃)₂) until a persistent lather is formed. The volume of soap required is recorded.

Observations: Distilled water lathers easily with very little soap. Water containing Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions requires a large volume of soap and forms scum.

Conclusion: Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions cause water hardness.

Revision Questions

Test your understanding with these 30 revision questions.

1. What is observed when magnesium reacts with dilute acids?

Effervescence (fizzing) is observed, indicating a gas is produced.

2. Why must a magnesium strip be cleaned before reacting it with acid?

To remove the protective outer layer of magnesium oxide (\(MgO\)), which would slow down or prevent the reaction.

3. What is the general word equation for the reaction between an active metal and a dilute acid?

Metal + Dilute Acid → Salt + Hydrogen Gas.

4. How is the strength of an acid related to its pH value?

The stronger the acid, the lower its pH value.

5. How does the electrical conductivity of an acid relate to its strength?

A stronger acid has a higher electrical conductivity because it produces more ions in solution.

6. Which is stronger: 2M hydrochloric acid or 2M ethanoic acid? Explain why.

Hydrochloric acid is stronger. It ionizes completely in water, producing a higher concentration of \(H^+\) ions, resulting in a lower pH and higher conductivity compared to ethanoic acid, which only ionizes partially.

7. Compare the pH values of 2M sodium hydroxide and 2M ammonia solution.

Sodium hydroxide solution has a higher pH value than ammonia solution.

8. What explains the difference in strength between sodium hydroxide and ammonia solution?

Sodium hydroxide is a strong alkali that dissociates completely in water to produce a high concentration of \(OH^-\) ions. Ammonia is a weak base that only partially reacts with water to produce a lower concentration of \(OH^-\) ions.

9. What is observed when dry hydrogen chloride gas is dissolved in methylbenzene and tested with litmus paper?

There is no observable change on the litmus paper because \(HCl\) does not ionize in the non-polar solvent.

10. Why does an aqueous solution of ammonia affect litmus paper?

Because ammonia reacts with water to form ammonium ions (\(NH_4^+\)) and hydroxide ions (\(OH^-\)). The hydroxide ions are responsible for turning red litmus paper blue.

11. Why does dry solid calcium hydroxide not affect dry litmus paper?

In the solid state, the ions in calcium hydroxide are held in a crystal lattice and are not free to move. There are no free \(OH^-\) ions to cause a color change.

12. Name a cation that forms an insoluble chloride.

Lead(II) (\(Pb^{2+}\)) and Silver (\(Ag^+\)) ions form insoluble chlorides.

13. Name two examples of insoluble sulphates.

Barium sulphate (\(BaSO_4\)) and lead(II) sulphate (\(PbSO_4\)).

14. Which three metal hydroxides are amphoteric and dissolve in excess sodium hydroxide solution?

Zinc hydroxide (\(Zn(OH)_2\)), aluminium hydroxide (\(Al(OH)_3\)), and lead(II) hydroxide (\(Pb(OH)_2\)).

15. Which common metal precipitate dissolves in excess ammonia solution to form a deep blue solution?

Copper(II) hydroxide (\(Cu(OH)_2\)).

16. Define solubility.

Solubility is the maximum mass of a solute that dissolves in 100 g of water to form a saturated solution at a specific temperature.

17. How does the solubility of most solid salts change with temperature?

The solubility of most solid salts increases as the temperature increases.

18. What is fractional crystallisation?

It is a method used to separate a mixture of soluble salts by taking advantage of their different solubilities at different temperatures.

19. Define hard water.

Hard water is water that does not lather readily with soap due to the presence of dissolved \(Ca^{2+}\) and/or \(Mg^{2+}\) ions.

20. What is the chemical name for scum?

Scum is an insoluble precipitate, typically calcium stearate or magnesium stearate, formed from the reaction between soap and hard water ions.

21. How can temporary hardness of water be removed?

By boiling the water, which causes the dissolved calcium hydrogencarbonate to precipitate as calcium carbonate.

22. Write an equation for the removal of temporary hardness by boiling.

\(Ca(HCO_3)_2(aq) \rightarrow CaCO_3(s) + H_2O(l) + CO_2(g)\)

23. Name one method for removing permanent hardness of water.

Using an ion exchanger or adding washing soda (sodium carbonate).

24. In the reaction \(NH_{3(g)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons NH_{4(aq)}^+ + OH^-_{(aq)}\), which substance acts as a base?

Ammonia (\(NH_3\)) acts as a base because it accepts a proton (\(H^+\)) from water.

25. Define a salt.

A salt is an ionic compound formed when the hydrogen ion of an acid is partially or completely replaced by a metal ion or an ammonium ion (\(NH_4^+\)).

26. Name a salt that can be prepared by precipitation.

Lead(II) chloride (\(PbCl_2\)), silver chloride (\(AgCl\)), or barium sulphate (\(BaSO_4\)).

27. Briefly describe how to prepare an insoluble salt like lead(II) sulphate.

By mixing two suitable soluble solutions, one containing lead(II) ions (e.g., lead(II) nitrate) and the other containing sulphate ions (e.g., sodium sulphate). The insoluble lead(II) sulphate forms as a precipitate, which can then be filtered, washed, and dried.

28. What is a base according to the Brønsted-Lowry theory?

A Brønsted-Lowry base is a proton (\(H^+\)) acceptor.

29. What is an acid according to the Brønsted-Lowry theory?

A Brønsted-Lowry acid is a proton (\(H^+\)) donor.

30. What is the pH of a neutral solution at 25°C?

The pH of a neutral solution is 7.
